## **2. The Nervous System: Reminder**

|Division|Components|Function|
|---|---|---|
|**Central Nervous System (CNS)**|Brain, spinal cord, optic nerve (CN II), retina|Integration, control, and coordination|
|**Peripheral Nervous System (PNS)**|Cranial and spinal nerves outside dura mater; sensory receptors; autonomic ganglia|Communication between CNS and body|

**Subtypes:**

- **Efferent (motor)**: signals _from_ CNS to effectors (muscle/glands).

## **4. Structure of a Neuron**

**Components:**

- **Dendrites:** receive incoming signals.
    
- **Cell body (soma):** metabolic centre, integrates signals.
    
- **Axon:** long projection transmitting impulses away from soma.
    
- **Myelin sheath:** insulating layer (Schwann cells or oligodendrocytes).
    
- **Nodes of Ranvier:** gaps between myelin ‚Üí allow signal regeneration.
    
- **Nerve terminal:** converts electrical signal to chemical neurotransmitter release.
    

**Signal Pathway:**

1. Impulse arrives via dendrites/cell body.
    
2. If threshold reached ‚Üí **action potential generated** at axon hillock.
    
3. Signal travels along axon ‚Üí nerve terminal ‚Üí neurotransmitter release to target (neuron, muscle, or gland).

## **6. Nerve Conduction ‚Äì The Basic Principle**

1. **Signal reception:** Input from dendrites/cell body.
    
2. **Integration:** If total depolarisation exceeds threshold ‚Üí AP generated.
    
3. **Transmission:** AP propagates down axon to terminals.
    
4. **Output:** Signal transmitted to next cell (neuron/muscle/gland).
    

---

## **7. Passive vs Active Conduction**

|Type|Mechanism|Analogy / Example|
|---|---|---|
|**Passive conduction**|Ion currents spread along membrane without new AP generation|Like heat moving along a metal rod|
|**Active conduction**|Regeneration of AP along membrane via voltage-gated ion channels|Self-propagating electrical impulse|

**Note:** Passive spread alone decays over distance ‚Äî hence **voltage-gated Na‚Å∫ channels** regenerate the AP at intervals.

---

## **8. Factors Affecting Conduction Speed**

- **Axon diameter:** larger = lower internal resistance ‚Üí faster conduction.
    
- **Capacitance:** ability of membrane to store charge; lower capacitance (as in myelinated axons) ‚Üí faster transmission.
    
- Speed cannot increase indefinitely by axon enlargement ‚Äî biological limit solved by **myelination**.
    

---

## **9. Resistance and Capacitance Concept**

- Membrane acts as a **capacitor**:
    
    - Two conductive fluids (ECF, ICF) separated by an insulating lipid membrane.
        
- For current to pass, **charge must overcome membrane capacitance**.
    

**Key Relation:**  
‚Üë Capacitance = ‚Üì conduction speed.  
‚Üí Myelin reduces capacitance ‚Üí faster signalling.

## **13. Electrical Excitability**

- **Depolarisation:** membrane potential becomes less negative (excitation).
    
- **Hyperpolarisation:** membrane potential becomes more negative (inhibition).
    
- **Resting potential:** maintained by **K‚Å∫ leak** and **Na‚Å∫/K‚Å∫ ATPase**.

## **14. Graded Potentials**

- Small, local depolarisations caused by opening of Na‚Å∫ channels.
    
- Amplitude depends on stimulus strength (‚Äúgraded‚Äù).
    
- **Decay** over distance (temporal & spatial).
    
- Multiple signals may **summate** if close in time/location.  
    ‚Üí ‚ÄúPassive conduction.‚Äù
    

üß† **Exam Focus:**  
Graded potentials are **additive**, **local**, and **decay** ‚Äî unlike self-propagating action potentials.

## **15. Generation of an Action Potential**

**Threshold Concept:**

- Multiple graded potentials summate ‚Üí reach **threshold (~ ‚Äì55 mV)** ‚Üí AP fired.
    

**Sequence of Events:**

1. **Depolarisation:** Na‚Å∫ channels open, Na‚Å∫ enters ‚Üí Vm becomes positive.
    
2. **Repolarisation:** Na‚Å∫ channels inactivate; K‚Å∫ channels open ‚Üí K‚Å∫ exits.
    
3. **Hyperpolarisation:** K‚Å∫ channels close slowly ‚Üí Vm becomes more negative.
    
4. **Return to Rest:** via K‚Å∫ leak and Na‚Å∫/K‚Å∫ ATPase activity.

## **16. Ionic Conductance Changes**

- AP has two major ionic phases:
    
    - **Na‚Å∫ influx ‚Üí depolarisation**
        
    - **K‚Å∫ efflux ‚Üí repolarisation/hyperpolarisation**
        
- Conductance shifts produce the waveform of an AP.

## **17. Propagation of the Action Potential**

### **A. Direction ‚Äì Refractory Periods**

- APs are **all-or-none** and **unidirectional**.
    
- After firing:
    
    - **Absolute refractory period:** no second AP possible (Na‚Å∫ channels inactivated).
        
    - **Relative refractory period:** stronger stimulus needed (partial recovery).
        
- Ensures **forward propagation only**.
    

üß† **Exam Focus:**  
Absolute = complete inactivation; Relative = partial recovery ‚Üí limits frequency.

---

### **B. Frequency**

- A **sustained suprathreshold stimulus** ‚Üí repetitive firing.
    
- Frequency encodes **stimulus intensity**.
    
- Different neuron types display distinct **spiking patterns** (e.g., rhythmic, bursting, adapting).
    

---

### **C. Speed ‚Äì Myelination**

- Myelin formed by:
    
    - **Oligodendrocytes (CNS)**
        
    - **Schwann cells (PNS)**
        
- Myelin increases conduction speed up to **10√ó**.
    
- Voltage-gated Na‚Å∫ channels cluster at **Nodes of Ranvier** ‚Üí APs **‚Äújump‚Äù** node-to-node = **Saltatory conduction**.
    

|Type|Speed|Conduction Mechanism|
|---|---|---|
|**Unmyelinated**|Slow|Continuous wave propagation|
|**Myelinated**|Fast|Saltatory (jumping) conduction|

## **18. Clinical Relevance ‚Äì Myelin Loss**

- **Demyelinating diseases** disrupt conduction.
    
- **Multiple Sclerosis (CNS):** autoimmune attack on oligodendrocytes ‚Üí impaired AP propagation ‚Üí weakness, sensory loss, fatigue.
    
- **Guillain-Barr√© Syndrome (PNS):** autoimmune demyelination of peripheral nerves ‚Üí paralysis, recovery possible.
    

üß† **Exam Focus:**  
Myelin = ‚Üë speed & efficiency; loss ‚Üí ‚Üì conduction, neurological deficits.
