## **2. mRNA Export**

- mRNA is synthesised in the **nucleus**, then exported to the **cytoplasm** via **nuclear pore complexes**.
    
- Export is **energy-dependent** (requires ATP/GTP).
    
- Once in cytoplasm ‚Üí mRNA binds **ribosomes** for translation.

## **3. The Genetic Code**

- **Triplet code:** 3 bases (codon) ‚Üí 1 amino acid.
    
- **Degenerate:** multiple codons can code for the same amino acid.
    
- **Non-overlapping:** read in sequential triplets from a fixed start point.
    
- **Universal:** used by almost all organisms.
    

### **Codons**

- **Start codon:** AUG (codes for **Methionine**) ‚Üí defines reading frame.
    
- **Stop codons:** UAA, UAG, UGA ‚Üí terminate translation.
    
- The **open reading frame (ORF)** lies between start and stop codons.
    

üß† **Exam Focus:**  
AUG = start; UAA, UAG, UGA = stop.

## **5. Types of Mutations**

|Type|Description|Example / Consequence|
|---|---|---|
|**Point mutation (substitution)**|One base replaced by another|Silent, missense, or nonsense|
|**Missense**|Single amino acid change|Sickle-cell disease (Glu‚ÜíVal)|
|**Nonsense**|Creates premature stop codon|Truncated protein|
|**Frameshift**|Insertion or deletion shifts reading frame|Often leads to non-functional protein|
|**Silent**|Base change, same amino acid (degenerate code)|No change in protein|
|**Polymorphism**|Common variation (>1 % pop.) with minor effect|May or may not affect phenotype|

üß† **Key distinction:**

- **Mutation:** alters phenotype / disease-causing. Only mutations affect gene function
    
- **Polymorphism:** common, may influence traits but usually not pathogenic, minor change in DNA sequence that is present in less than 1% of population

## **6. tRNA ‚Äì The Adaptor Molecule**

- tRNA links **codon (mRNA)** to **specific amino acid**.
    
- Each tRNA carries a **specific anticodon** that base-pairs with the mRNA codon.
    
    - Example: mRNA codon GAC ‚Üí tRNA anticodon CUG ‚Üí Aspartate.
        

### **Structure of tRNA**

- **Anticodon loop:** base-pairs with mRNA codon.
    
- **3‚Ä≤ end (CCA):** attachment site for amino acid.
    
- **‚ÄúWobble‚Äù base-pairing** allows flexibility (one tRNA can recognise several codons).

## **8. Ribosomes and Translation**

### **Ribosome Structure**

- Made of **rRNA + proteins**.
    
- Two subunits (large + small).
- For every one binding site for mRNA, there are three for tRNA
    
- **Three tRNA binding sites:**
    
    - **A site (Aminoacyl)** ‚Äì incoming charged tRNA.
        
    - **P site (Peptidyl)** ‚Äì tRNA holding growing chain.
        
    - **E site (Exit)** ‚Äì tRNA leaves ribosome.
        

---

## **9. Stages of Translation**

|Stage|Description|Key Components|
|---|---|---|
|**1. Initiation**|Small subunit binds mRNA 5‚Ä≤ cap; scans to find AUG start codon.|Initiation factors, GTP, initiator tRNA (Met)|
|**2. Elongation**|Ribosome catalyses peptide bonds between amino acids; moves one codon forward.|Elongation factors, peptidyl transferase (rRNA enzyme)|
|**3. Termination**|Stop codon reached; release factors break final tRNA‚Äìpolypeptide bond.|Release factors, GTP|

**Polysomes (polyribosomes):** multiple ribosomes translating one mRNA simultaneously ‚Üí efficient protein synthesis.

üß† **Exam Focus:**

- Start codon = AUG.
    
- Peptide bonds formed between **C-terminal of P-site AA** and **N-terminal of A-site AA**.
    
- Termination requires **release factors** (not tRNA).

## **10. Antibiotic Inhibition of Translation**

Some antibiotics block translation in bacteria (basis of selective toxicity):

| Antibiotic          | Target      | Mechanism                                                          |
| ------------------- | ----------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Tetracycline**    | 30S subunit | Blocks tRNA binding                                                |
| **Chloramphenicol** | 50S subunit | Inhibits peptidyl transferase (inhibits formation of peptide bond) |
| **Erythromycin**    | 50S subunit | Blocks translocation                                               |
| **Streptomycin**    | 30S subunit | Misreads mRNA                                                      |

## **11. What Happens After Translation?**

### **Protein Folding**

- Begins **co-translationally** on ribosome.
    
- Assisted by **chaperone proteins (Hsp70, Hsp90)**.
    

### **Post-Translational Modifications (PTMs)**

|Modification|Example / Function|
|---|---|
|**Phosphorylation**|Activates/inactivates enzymes (Ser, Thr, Tyr)|
|**Glycosylation (Asn-X-Ser/Thr)**|Secreted/membrane proteins|
|**Hydroxylation (Lys, Pro)**|Collagen stability|
|**Acetylation / Methylation**|Histone regulation|
|**Ubiquitination**|Marks proteins for degradation|

üß† **Clinical Link:**  
**Tau hyperphosphorylation** ‚Üí neurofibrillary tangles in **Alzheimer‚Äôs disease**.  
Both too little and too much modification cause pathology. Tau protein is the microtubule-associated protein that is abundant in the neurons

## **12. Protein Targeting and Transport**

|Destination|Signal Sequence / Pathway|
|---|---|
|**Cytoplasm**|No signal sequence|
|**Nucleus**|Nuclear localisation signal (NLS)|
|**ER / Secretory pathway**|N-terminal signal peptide ‚Üí directs to rough ER|
|**Mitochondria**|N-terminal mitochondrial import sequence|

- **Signal peptides** may be **cleaved** after transport.
    
- Proteins may **shuttle between compartments**.
    
- Proper targeting ensures correct localisation and function.
