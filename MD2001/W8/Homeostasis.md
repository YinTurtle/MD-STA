## **1. Definition of Homeostasis**

- Maintenance of a **‚Äúnearly constant‚Äù internal environment**.
    
- Essential for **cellular and organ function**.
    
- Involves **integration** of all systems (nervous, endocrine, cardiovascular, etc.).
    
- **Disruption beyond safe limits** ‚Üí impaired cellular function ‚Üí disease ‚Üí death (if severe).

## **3. Cells and Basic Cellular Functions**

- Enclosed by a **lipid bilayer membrane**.
    
- Specialised by function (e.g., RBCs = O‚ÇÇ transport; neurons = signal transmission).
    
- Common features:
    
    - Use **oxygen** for energy.
        
    - Convert **nutrients ‚Üí ATP**.
        
    - Deliver **waste ‚Üí extracellular fluid (ECF)**.
        
    - Maintain selective **transport in/out** via membranes.
        
    - Replicate and repair.

## **6. Fluid Balance**

- **Intake = Output** in steady-state physiology.
    
- Water input: food, drink, metabolism.
    
- Output: urine, sweat, breath, faeces.
    
- Regulation achieved by **renal, endocrine, and thirst mechanisms**.

## **7. Physiological Control Systems**

- Maintain variables within a **normal range** (safe limits).
    
- Operate through **self-regulating feedback mechanisms**.
    

**Types of control:**

- **Nervous system:** rapid (milliseconds‚Äìseconds), short duration.
    
- **Endocrine system:** slower (minutes‚Äìhours), longer duration.
    
- **Genetic/cellular** regulation occurs within tissues and cells.
    

---

## **8. Control System Types**

|System Type|Feedback?|Description|
|---|---|---|
|**Open-loop**|‚ùå None|Output not affected by outcome (e.g., gut glucose absorption)|
|**Closed-loop**|‚úÖ Yes|Output feeds back to controller ‚Äî maintains homeostasis|

### **Examples**

- **Open-loop:** Radiator on a timer (no temperature sensing).
    
- **Closed-loop:** Thermostat system (senses temperature, adjusts heating).
    

**Exam Focus:**

## **9. Negative Feedback (Most Common)**

- Returns variable **toward set point**.
    
- Maintains **stability**.
    
- Example: **Thermoregulation**
    

**Elements of a negative feedback loop:**

1. **Sensor (receptor):** detects change.
    
2. **Control centre:** compares input to set point.
    
3. **Effector:** produces response to restore normality.
    

**Example: Thermoregulation**

- **‚Üë Body Temp:** hypothalamus ‚Üí sweating, vasodilation ‚Üí ‚Üì T.
    
- **‚Üì Body Temp:** hypothalamus ‚Üí shivering, vasoconstriction ‚Üí ‚Üë T.
    

**Clinical Focus:**  
Failure ‚Üí **hyperthermia** or **hypothermia**.

---

## **10. Hunting in Negative Feedback**

- Slight oscillation (‚Äúhunting‚Äù) occurs around the set point due to delay between detection and response.
    
- The system is dynamic, not fixed.
    
- **Set point can shift** in fever, exercise, or acclimatisation (e.g., high altitude).
    

**Term:**  
‚ÄúGain‚Äù = degree of system effectiveness ‚Üí _Gain = Correction / Error_.

- High gain ‚Üí more precise control (e.g., thermoregulation gain ‚âà 27).
    

---

## **11. Example: Blood Pressure Regulation**

**Sensor:** baroreceptors (carotid sinus, aortic arch) detect pressure changes.  
**Control centre:** medulla oblongata.  
**Effectors:** heart & blood vessels.

‚Üí If BP rises ‚Üí reflex bradycardia + vasodilation ‚Üí ‚Üì BP.  
‚Üí If BP falls ‚Üí tachycardia + vasoconstriction ‚Üí ‚Üë BP.

**Clinical Focus:**

- Impaired baroreflex (e.g., in diabetes, ageing) ‚Üí BP instability or fainting.

## **12. Positive Feedback**

- Output **enhances** original stimulus ‚Üí drives system away from set point.
    
- **Usually needs a termination mechanism** to prevent ‚Äúrunaway‚Äù effect.
    

**Physiological examples:**

1. **Action potential:** Na‚Å∫ entry depolarises membrane, opens more Na‚Å∫ channels.
    
2. **Childbirth:** Oxytocin ‚Üë uterine contractions ‚Üí more oxytocin ‚Üí birth stops cycle.
    
3. **Blood clotting:** Platelet activation ‚Üí more clotting factors ‚Üí clot formation halts process.
    

**Exam Focus:**  
Know at least 2 physiological examples of positive feedback and their termination mechanisms.

---

## **13. Feedforward Control**

- Response triggered **in anticipation** of change (no feedback).
    
- Example:
    
    - Sight/smell of food ‚Üí salivation, gastric acid secretion.
        
    - Prepares GI tract ‚Üí maintains glucose & nutrient homeostasis during digestion.
        

**Clinical Focus:**  
Feedforward failure may impair adaptation (e.g., insulin release delay in diabetes).

---

## **14. Biological Rhythms & Set Point Variability**

- **Set points fluctuate** with circadian or environmental changes.
    
    - Example: cortisol peaks in morning, lowest at midnight.
        
    - Temperature lowest during sleep.
        
- Reflects adaptive, predictable variation within homeostasis.
    

---

## **15. Summary of Feedback Types**

|Feedback Type|Function|Example|Result|
|---|---|---|---|
|**Negative**|Returns to normal range|BP, temperature|Stability|
|**Positive**|Amplifies change|Childbirth, clotting|Instability (requires termination)|
|**Feedforward**|Anticipates change|Digestion, movement|Improved control|

## **16. Overall Principles**

- **Homeostasis = stability through control.**
    
- Requires coordinated actions of **nervous + endocrine systems**.
    
- **Loss of homeostasis ‚Üí disease.**
