## **1. Overview**

- The endocrine system is the **second major control system** of the body (after the nervous system).

- Works more **slowly** but has longer-lasting effects. (contrast to the nervous system)

- **Hormones:** chemical messengers secreted into blood ‚Üí act on distant target cells.

- Releasing hormones into the blood system - targeting specific cells

## **2. Endocrine vs Exocrine Glands**

Endocrine glands - ductless glands with rich blood supply
	Endo = internal, crine = secretion
Typically act on tissues distant from the source

#### **Types of endocrine hormones**

Paracrine
- Signal when a cell signals to nearby cells by releasing a chemical that diffuses to target neighboring cells

Autocrine
- Signal when a cell targets itself by releasing a chemical that binds to its own receptors

## **3. Classes of Hormones**

Proteins/peptides, steroids, amino acid derivatives

### **(1) Peptide / Protein Hormones**

- Chains of amino acids; water-soluble (readily transported in blood).
    
- **Short half-life** (minutes).
    
- Bind **cell membrane receptors** ‚Üí activate intracellular signalling (i.e. gene expression).
    
- Examples: **Insulin**, **GH**, **TSH**, **ADH**.
    ![[Pasted image 20251030141721.png]]

### **(2) Steroid Hormones**

- Derived from **cholesterol**; lipid-soluble.
    
- Require **carrier proteins** in blood ‚Üí longer half-life.
    
- Cross membranes ‚Üí bind **intracellular receptors**, alter **gene expression**.
    
- Examples: **Cortisol**, **Aldosterone**, **Testosterone**, **Oestrogen**.

![[Pasted image 20251030141934.png]]


### **(3) Amino Acid Derivatives**

- Derived from **tyrosine**.
    
- Two subtypes:
    
    - **Catecholamines:** Adrenaline, Noradrenaline, Dopamine (water-soluble).
	    - hormones when secreted from adrenal medulla, neurotransmitters within the nervous system
        
    - **Thyroid hormones:** T‚ÇÉ, T‚ÇÑ (lipid-soluble).
    
Although they have a common precursor - catecholamines behave very differently to thyroid hormones.
		- e.g. properties: function, half life etc

**Exam Focus:** Match hormone examples to their chemical class.

## **4. Hormone Targets and Mechanisms**

- Hormones act via **specific receptors** on/in target cells:
    
    - **Cell Membrane receptors:** for peptides, glycoproteins & catecholamines.
        
    - **Intracellular receptors:** for steroids & thyroid hormones.
    
- Hormones act in **very low concentrations** and are released:
    
    - **Episodically:** response to physiological demand
	    - e.g., insulin after eating.
        
    - **Diurnally:** predictable pattern over 24h
	    - e.g., cortisol (peak 6‚Äì8 AM).
	    - may also response to episodic stimuli
        
    - **Constantly:** fairly constant blood concentrations for normal physiological function
	    - e.g., thyroxine (steady baseline).
    ![[Pasted image 20251030142422.png]]
    Episodic - white
	Diurnal - red
	Constant - green

**Clinical Focus:**  
Disruption of release rhythm ‚Üí fatigue, metabolic or mood disturbances.

## **6. Endocrine Regulation ‚Äì Hypothalamic‚ÄìPituitary Axis**

- The **hypothalamus and pituitary gland** control most endocrine activity.
    
- **Signal cascade:** Hypothalamus ‚Üí Pituitary ‚Üí Target gland ‚Üí Target cells.
    
- Controlled by **negative feedback loops** (hormone levels inhibit upstream release).
    

**Example:**  
TRH (hypothalamus) ‚Üí TSH (pituitary) ‚Üí T‚ÇÉ/T‚ÇÑ (thyroid) ‚Üí inhibits TRH/TSH.
![[Pasted image 20251030143104.png]]

**Clinical Focus:**

- Tumours or damage can cause hormone excess or deficiency (e.g., pituitary adenoma).

## **7. Hypothalamus**

- Produces **releasing hormones** (e.g., GHRH, TRH, CRH, GnRH) and **inhibitory hormones** (e.g., GHIH) from the **anterior lobe** of the pituitary gland
    
- Controls **anterior pituitary** via blood (portal) system.
    
- Synthesises **ADH** and **Oxytocin**, stored in the **posterior lobe** of the pituitary gland.

## **9. Thyroid Gland**

- Two lobes on either side of **trachea**, connected by **isthmus**. Covering the anterolateral surface of the trachea
- Largest pure endocrine gland

![[Pasted image 20251030144509.png]]
    
- **Rich blood supply**, innervated by autonomic nerves.
    
- Derived embryologically from tongue epithelium.

![[Pasted image 20251030144520.png]]    

### **Hormones:**

- **T‚ÇÉ (Tri-iodothyronine)** and **T‚ÇÑ (Thyroxine)** from **follicular cells** - stored in follicle lumen.
    
- **Calcitonin** (from parafollicular C cells) ‚Üí ‚Üì blood Ca¬≤‚Å∫.
    
![[Pasted image 20251030144612.png]]
### **Functions of T‚ÇÉ/T‚ÇÑ**

- ‚Üë Basal metabolic rate, O‚ÇÇ use, protein synthesis, and heat production.
    
- Essential for **growth, brain development**, and **tissue differentiation**.
    

**Clinical Focus:**

| Disorder                             | Cause                        | Features                                   |
| ------------------------------------ | ---------------------------- | ------------------------------------------ |
| **Hypothyroidism / goitre**          | Iodine deficiency            | Weight gain, fatigue, cold intolerance     |
| **Hyperthyroidism (Graves disease)** | Autoimmune                   | Weight loss, tachycardia, heat intolerance |
| **Cretinism / CIDS**                 | Congenital iodine deficiency | Growth & neurological delay                |

- Thyroxine regulates
	- rate of metabolism - energy use
	- protein production - growth and development

## **11. Adrenal (Suprarenal) Glands**

- Sit atop each kidney; each has **cortex** + **medulla**.

|---|---|---|
|**Zona glomerulosa**|**Aldosterone** (mineralocorticoid)|Regulates Na‚Å∫, K‚Å∫, water ‚Üí BP control|

**Clinical Focus:**

#### **Negative Feedback**

Hypothalamic - pituitary - adrenal axis

- cortisol feedback mechanism

	- lowest: midnight - 2am
- Main effects:
	- glucose mobilisation
	- cardiovascular (blood pressure)

### **B. Adrenal Medulla**

- Derived from **neural crest - neuroectoderm** (sympathetic ganglia).
    
- Produces **catecholamines:** adrenaline, noradrenaline, dopamine.
    
- ‚ÄúFight or Flight‚Äù responses: ‚Üë HR, BP, respiration, glucose; redirects blood to muscles.

	- Where adrenaline and noradrenaline are synthesized
- Site of catecholamine synthesis and secretion
- Chromaffin cells produce
	- epinephrine
	- norepinephrine
	- dopamine
- Key effects:
	- increase in heart rate, bp and respiratory rate
	- diversion of blood to muscles
	- glucose mobilisation
![[Pasted image 20251030145842.png|600]]

**Clinical Focus:**  
**Phaeochromocytoma** ‚Äì tumour of chromaffin cells ‚Üí episodic hypertension, palpitations.

**Exam Focus:**  
Differentiate cortex (steroids) vs medulla (catecholamines).
