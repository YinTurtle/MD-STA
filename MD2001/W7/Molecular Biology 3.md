## **3. Transcription Overview**

- **DNA template strand** is used to make **complementary RNA**.
    
- **RNA polymerase** synthesizes RNA **5‚Ä≤‚Üí3‚Ä≤**, reading the template **3‚Ä≤‚Üí5‚Ä≤**.
    
- No primer needed (unlike DNA polymerase).
    
- Less accurate than DNA replication, but errors are less harmful since RNA is short-lived.
    
- **Many RNA copies** can be made from one gene at once.
    

üß† **Key Concept:**  
Transcription is selective ‚Äî not all genes are transcribed at once; regulation controls which genes are active.

## **5. Promoters and Transcription Initiation**

### **Promoter**

- Region **upstream of transcription start site** where RNA polymerase binds.
    
- **Core promoter** (e.g., TATA box ~25 bp upstream): binds **TBP (TATA-binding protein)** ‚Üí bends DNA.
    
- **Proximal promoter** (CAAT, GC boxes): binds general transcription factors (TFIIB, TFIID, TFIIF, etc.).
    
- **Housekeeping genes** often have **GC boxes** instead of TATA boxes.
    

üß† **Focus:**  
Promoters determine **where transcription starts** and **how strongly** a gene is expressed.

---

## **6. Enhancers**

- **Short DNA sequences** that increase transcription efficiency.
    
- Can be **upstream or downstream**, **close or far**, and **orientation-independent**.
    
- Bind **transcription factors** ‚Üí help assemble transcription machinery.
    
- Work through **DNA looping** or **scanning** mechanisms.
    
- **Silencers** have the opposite effect (repress transcription).
    

üí° **Analogy:**  
Promoters are the ‚Äúdoor‚Äù where transcription begins; enhancers are the ‚Äúloudspeakers‚Äù that make it happen faster.

---

## **7. Transcription Factors**

- Contain **two functional domains**:
    
    1. **DNA-binding domain** ‚Äì recognizes specific sequences (TATA, GC box, etc.).
        
    2. **Activation domain** ‚Äì interacts with transcription machinery or coactivators.
        

üß† **Signal Response:**  
Many transcription factors are **signal-dependent**, e.g., steroid hormone receptors, which bind enhancers only when the hormone is present.

## **8. RNA Processing (Post-Transcriptional Modifications)**

After transcription, **pre-mRNA (hnRNA)** must be modified before it becomes **mature mRNA**.

### **a. 5‚Ä≤ Capping**

- Addition of **7-methylguanosine (m‚Å∑G)** to 5‚Ä≤ end.
    
- Protects mRNA from degradation and helps ribosome recognition.
    

### **b. Splicing**

- **Introns** (non-coding regions) are removed, and **exons** (coding sequences) joined.
    
- Mediated by **spliceosome** (contains snRNPs = snRNA + protein).
    
- Occurs via **two transesterification reactions** forming a **lariat** intermediate.
    
- **Splice sites** recognized by conserved GU (5‚Ä≤) and AG (3‚Ä≤) sequences.
    

üí° **Focus:**  
Introns do not encode protein but allow regulation and diversity via alternative splicing.

### **c. 3‚Ä≤ Polyadenylation**

- Addition of **poly(A) tail (~100‚Äì250 adenines)**.
    
- Enhances stability, nuclear export, and translation efficiency.
    

---

## **9. Alternative Splicing**

- **Same gene ‚Üí multiple mRNAs ‚Üí multiple proteins**.
    
- Exon order maintained, but inclusion/exclusion varies.
    
- Generates **protein isoforms** with distinct functions.
    

üß¨ **Clinical Example:**

- **Frasier syndrome:** defective alternative splicing in WT1 gene ‚Üí abnormal kidney development (glomerulonephropathy).
    

üß† **Exam Tip:**  
Alternative splicing helps explain the **‚Äúgene number paradox‚Äù** ‚Äî humans make ~100,000 proteins from ~20,000 genes.

---

## **10. Gene Number Paradox**

**Answer:** C ‚Äî _Making many different proteins from a limited number of genes._

üîπ Mechanisms increasing protein diversity:

- Alternative **promoters**
    
- Alternative **splicing**
    
- Alternative **3‚Ä≤ ends**
    
- **RNA editing**
    
- **Post-translational modifications**
