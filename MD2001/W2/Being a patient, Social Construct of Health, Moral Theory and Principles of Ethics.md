# **2.6 Medicalisation (Slides 28‚Äì32)**

Medicalisation = defining normal experiences as medical problems.

Examples:

- **Death** ‚Üí moved from family rituals ‚Üí hospitalised, professionalised
    
- **Menopause** ‚Üí increasingly framed as pathology requiring HRT

**Over-medicalisation impacts:**

- Unnecessary procedures
    
- Cost burden
    
- Loss of autonomy
    
- Stigma and labelling
    
- Pharmaceutical dependency

# **3.1 What is Ethics? (Slides 1‚Äì3)**

Ethics = study of how we ought to live.  
Medical ethics = applying this to clinical decisions.

---

# **3.2 Three Main Moral Theories (Slides 7‚Äì15)**

## **A. Deontology ‚Äî Kant**

- Duty-based
    
- Follow rules regardless of consequences
    
- Example: not lying to protect Jeff
    
- Strength: clarity, consistency
    
- Weakness: rigid, ignores outcomes
    

## **B. Consequentialism ‚Äî Bentham, Mill**

- Right action = best outcome
    
- Utilitarianism: maximise pleasure/minimise pain
    
- Vaccine example: population benefit > rare risks
    
- Weakness: can justify harming individuals
    

## **C. Virtue Ethics ‚Äî Aristotle**

- Right action = what a virtuous person would do
    
- Focuses on character (caring, trustworthy, disciplined)
    
- Weakness: vague, culturally variable
    

---

# **3.3 Limitations (Slide 17)**

No single theory works always ‚Üí ethics uses **all as lenses**.

---

# **3.4 Four Principles of Medical Ethics (Slides 18‚Äì33)**

## **1. Autonomy**

- Right to make decisions (even irrational ones)
    
- Competent adult can refuse any treatment, even life-saving
    
- JW example (refusal of transfusion)
    

## **2. Beneficence**

- Act to benefit the patient
    
- Conflicts with autonomy when patient choices differ
    

## **3. Non-maleficence**

- ‚ÄúDo no harm‚Äù
    
- Links to Doctrine of Double Effect
    

## **4. Justice**

- Fair distribution of healthcare resources
    
- Equivalent needs ‚Üí equivalent access
    

---

# **3.5 Doctrine of Double Effect (Slides 24‚Äì31)**

Action with both good and bad effects is permissible ONLY if:

1. Action itself is good
    
2. Intention is good
    
3. Bad effect is not a means to the good effect
    
4. Good outweighs bad
    

Classic example:

- High-dose morphine for pain relief even though it may hasten death

# üîó **INTERLINKING: How These Three Topics Fit Together Naturally**

### **1. Patient Experience ‚Üî Social Construction**

- How patients **interpret** diagnoses (shock, fear, avoidance) is shaped by culture and stigma.
    
- E.g., prostate cancer ‚Üí embarrassment among male relatives; HIV stigma ‚Üí avoidance of care.
    

### **2. Patient Experience ‚Üî Ethics**

- Communication quality (Being a Patient slides) links to **respect for autonomy**, **beneficence**, and **non-maleficence**.
    
- Poorly delivered bad news can inflict harm ‚Üí ethical failure.
    
- Ignoring psychosocial distress breaches beneficence.
    

### **3. Social Construction ‚Üî Ethics**

- Autonomy requires understanding the **patient‚Äôs cultural meaning** of illness.
    
- Justice demands awareness of **social inequities** (e.g., racialised pain myths).
    
- Over-medicalisation raises questions about **non-maleficence**.
    

### **4. All Three ‚Üî Better Clinical Practice**

Together they emphasise:

- Patient-centred care
    
- Cultural humility
    
- Thoughtful ethical reasoning
    
- Non-judgmental communication
    
- Awareness of stigma and social context
