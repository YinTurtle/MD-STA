## 1. What is Radiology?

**Definition:**  
Radiology is the branch of medicine that uses _radiant energy_ (such as X-rays, radio waves, ultrasound, or radioactive materials) to **diagnose and treat disease**.

|Role|Description|
|---|---|
|**Radiologist**|Doctor who interprets imaging (X-ray, CT, MRI) and performs image-guided procedures (e.g. biopsies, angiography).|
|**Radiographer**|Professional who performs the imaging. Those trained in ultrasound are called **Sonographers**.|
|**Clinical Oncologist**|Uses radiation to treat cancer (e.g. megavoltage X-rays, radionuclides).|

## 6. Computed Tomography (CT)

**Principle:**  
CT takes many X-rays in a circle and uses computers to make detailed **cross-sectional slices** (like slicing a loaf of bread).

**Advantages**

- Fast and widely available.
    
- Excellent for **bone, lung, trauma, bleeding**.
    
- 3D reconstruction possible.
    

**Disadvantages**

- **High radiation dose** (â‰ˆ 100â€“300 chest X-rays).
    
- Poorer soft tissue contrast than MRI.
    
- Expensive.

## 7. Nuclear Medicine (Scintigraphy, SPECT, PET)

**Principle:**  
A **radiopharmaceutical** (radioactive tracer + biological molecule) is injected â†’ emits **gamma rays** â†’ detected by a **gamma camera** â†’ computer builds the image.

|Technique|Principle|Example|
|---|---|---|
|**Scintigraphy**|Detects gamma rays from tracers like Technetium-99m|Bone scan for metastases|
|**SPECT**|3D reconstruction of scintigraphy|Cardiac perfusion imaging|
|**PET (Positron Emission Tomography)**|Uses positron-emitting isotopes, often combined with CT|Cancer staging (FDG-PET)|

**Pros:** Shows _function_ (metabolic activity).  
**Cons:** High radiation dose, limited anatomical detail.

## 8. Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI)

**Principle:**

- Uses a **powerful magnet** to align hydrogen atoms.
    
- **Radiofrequency pulses** disturb this alignment; returning signals are converted into images.
    
- **No ionising radiation.**

**Advantages**

- Best for **brain, spine, joints, soft tissue**.
    
- No radiation.
    
- Excellent differentiation between pathologies.
    

**Disadvantages**

- Expensive and time-consuming.
    
- Unsafe for metal implants or pacemakers.
    
- Noisy; may need sedation in children.

## 9. Ultrasound (US)

**Principle:**  
High-frequency **sound waves** reflect differently off tissues â†’ returning echoes form an image.

**Advantages**

- No radiation â€“ safe in **pregnancy**.
    
- Real-time imaging (movement, blood flow).
    
- Portable and cheap.
    

**Disadvantages**

- **Operator-dependent**.
    
- Poor for **bone or air-filled structures** (e.g. lungs, bowel).
    
- May be limited by obesity or overlying gas.

## 10. Radiation Safety & Guidelines

**Principle: ALARA â€“ â€œAs Low As Reasonably Achievableâ€**  
â†’ Always minimise radiation while achieving diagnostic goals.

**IRMER (UK Law):**  
Ionising Radiation (Medical Exposure) Regulations â€“ ensure justification, optimisation, and limitation of radiation use.

**Before Requesting Imaging:**

1. Will the result **change management**?
    
2. Is there a **safer alternative** (e.g. ultrasound or MRI)?
    
3. Has the test **already been done** recently?
    
4. Is the patient **pregnant** or possibly pregnant?

## 11. Common Pitfalls in Imaging Requests

- Repeating unnecessary tests.
    
- Ordering the **wrong investigation** (e.g. bone scan instead of CT).
    
- Not including enough **clinical detail** (radiologists need context).
    
- Over-investigation â†’ overdiagnosis, anxiety, and cost.
